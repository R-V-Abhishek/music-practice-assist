Based on the research literature outlined in your presentation document, here are detailed summaries for each of the 15 foundational papers. 

### 1. Hebbar & Jagtap (2023) - A Comparison of Audio Preprocessing Techniques and Deep Learning Algorithms
This research focuses on the foundational step of audio preprocessing for music classification tasks. By converting raw audio into Mel-spectrogram representations, the study leverages 2D Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) to analyze spectral patterns. The authors trained their models using the CMD-40 dataset, which contains 480 songs across 40 ragas. The model successfully learns the spatial and spectral layouts of different melodic patterns, achieving a robust 98.1% accuracy in raga classification. The primary strength of this approach lies in its high accuracy and ability to learn distinct raga signatures without relying on manually engineered acoustic features. However, the study's limitation is its lack of microtonal (shruti-level) precision, which the Automated Music Teacher project explicitly addresses by adding custom 22-shruti quantization to the processing pipeline.

### 2. Krishnaswamy (2018) - Application of Pitch Tracking to South Indian Classical Music
Krishnaswamy explores the fundamental task of pitch tracking specifically adapted for South Indian Classical music. Since Carnatic music fundamentally relies on a relative tonic (Sa) rather than fixed Western absolute pitches, standard pitch detection algorithms often fail to capture the necessary nuances. The study utilizes Short-Time Fourier Transforms (STFT) to execute highly accurate pitch tracking directly on Carnatic recordings. A core contribution of this work is the reliable extraction of the fundamental frequency (F0) from complex acoustic signals. This is incredibly relevant for automated tonic detection systems, as establishing an accurate F0 is the absolute prerequisite for all subsequent microtonal mapping. The Automated Music Teacher project incorporates these findings to establish its foundational pitch processing pipeline while systematically correcting for standard Western 12-tone biases.

### 3. Peri (2023) - Applying NLP and Deep Learning Techniques for Raga Recognition
Peri introduces a novel paradigm by treating raga recognition as a document classification problem. Recognizing that Indian Classical Music is highly structured, the study hierarchically processes digital audio sequences similar to textual sentences in Natural Language Processing. By utilizing a Hierarchical Attention Network, the methodology effectively isolates and models distinct note sequences. The models were trained and validated on extensive datasets, including Carnatic Music Datasets (CMD) and Hindustani Music Datasets (HMD). The system uses temporal sequence modeling to identify complex grammar patterns within the music. This approach is invaluable because it provides a computational method to validate grammar against Arohana-Avarohana rules, although the paper primarily focuses on classification. The Automated Music Teacher extends this exact concept by utilizing attention mechanisms to actively validate live student performances against specific raga grammar constraints.

### 4. Natesan & Beigi (2020) - Carnatic Raga Identification System using Rigorous TDNN
Natesan and Beigi address the complex challenge of large-scale raga identification using advanced temporal modeling. The paper leverages a hybrid architecture combining Time-Delay Neural Networks (TDNN), Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) models, and attention mechanisms to process acoustic data. Tested on a substantial dataset of 676 songs spanning 172 distinct ragas, the system achieved a classification accuracy of 95.31%. The neural network is explicitly designed to trace the intricate melodic patterns of Arohana and Avarohana over time, making it highly effective for identifying structural motifs within Carnatic pieces. The attention mechanisms used in this study heavily highlight critical musical phrases, providing a robust foundation for grammar validation. Your project adapts these findings by extending the temporal modeling logic beyond simple classification, focusing instead on real-time pedagogical rule-checking for students.

### 5. Carnatic Classical Raga Identification Using Stacked Ensemble Learning Model (2022)
This research paper focuses on using a Stacked Ensemble Learning Model to accurately identify complex Carnatic ragas. Since a single machine learning model often struggles with the intricate and overlapping features of Indian classical music, an ensemble approach combines the predictive power of multiple distinct algorithms. By intelligently aggregating various features, the ensemble model significantly improves overall classification robustness. The research demonstrates that stacking classifiers minimizes the variance and bias present in individual predictive models. The methodology underscores the absolute necessity of multi-modal feature extraction. This concept is directly relevant to the Automated Music Teacher project, which employs a multi-modal feature fusion approach—combining Mel-spectrograms, F0 contours, Chroma features, and 22-shruti quantization—to ensure high-fidelity raga identification and structural validation.

### 6. Comparative Study of the Working of Various AI Classifiers for Identifying Ragas (2021)
Presented at the International Conference on Computational Intelligence and Data Science, this paper systematically compares various artificial intelligence classifiers used for raga identification. The study thoroughly benchmarks the performance of traditional machine learning algorithms alongside modern deep learning frameworks when exposed to complex Indian classical music datasets. The authors meticulously document the overall accuracy, computational efficiency, and processing latency of each tested model. This comparative analysis is essential for identifying which algorithms are best suited for real-time applications where low latency is critical. The Automated Music Teacher project directly benefits from this type of architectural analysis, as the system strictly requires a framework capable of classifying ragas and detecting microtonal errors with a latency of under 50ms. The insights heavily justify the project's selection of lightweight models that satisfy real-time operational constraints.

### 7. Deep Learning-Based Classification of Indian Classical Music Based on Raga (2022)
Published in the IEEE Transactions on Multimedia, this paper extensively explores the application of deep neural networks for categorizing Indian classical music by raga. It transitions away from manually engineered audio features, advocating instead for the raw spectral processing capabilities of Deep Convolutional Neural Networks (DCNNs). The model processes large-scale, high-resolution audio spectrograms to automatically extract deep, hierarchical features that closely correspond to nuanced melodic movements and characteristic musical phrases. The research strongly emphasizes that deep learning architectures are highly capable of mapping the nonlinear relationships inherent in raga structures. This foundational, data-driven approach is directly mirrored in the Automated Music Teacher project's first development phase, which relies heavily on a 1D-DCNN classifier trained on the CMD-40 dataset to achieve its target raga identification of over 95% accuracy.

### 8. Explainable Deep Learning Analysis for Raga Identification in Indian Art Music (2023)
Presented at the ISMIR 2023 conference, this study actively tackles the "black box" nature of deep learning models in the context of Indian Art Music. Explainable AI (XAI) techniques are directly applied to raga identification systems to deeply understand what specific musical elements the neural networks are focusing on when making their predictions. The researchers meticulously investigate whether the deep learning model actually aligns with human expert understanding—for instance, verifying if the model heavily weights signature gamakas (oscillations) and crucial anchor notes. For a pedagogical tool like the Automated Music Teacher, conceptual explainability is absolutely crucial. The underlying insights from this paper conceptually support the project's ultimate goal of not just classifying music blindly, but providing objective, transparent assessment and real-time visual feedback that human students and teachers can inherently trust and logically understand.

### 9. Investigation into the Identification of Ragas: A Detailed Analysis (2021)
Published in the Journal of the Acoustical Society of India, this detailed analysis delves deeply into the underlying acoustic principles and exact feature sets strictly required for robust raga identification. The thorough investigation provides a comprehensive breakdown of exactly how different ragas exhibit entirely unique statistical distributions of pitch and energy over time. The authors analyze the behavior of various swara combinations and their frequency of occurrence, highlighting the undeniable importance of structural context in melodic identification. This paper inherently acts as a critical reference point for understanding the deep domain knowledge required to build specialized music information retrieval systems for Indian music. The conceptual definitions of scale patterns, vivaadi swaras (forbidden notes), and the necessity of the 22-shruti framework detailed in your presentation's domain knowledge section heavily draw upon the foundational acoustic analyses presented in rigorous literature like this.

### 10. Melody Extraction from Polyphonic Music by Deep Learning (2020)
This ISMIR 2020 paper specifically focuses on the highly complex task of isolating a single melodic line from a dense polyphonic audio mixture using Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN). The detailed research highlights a data-driven separation technique fully capable of real-time melody tracking within complex acoustic environments. The advanced model effectively tracks the continuous fundamental frequency (F0) of the lead melody, clearly differentiating it from overlapping accompanying instruments. While this specific paper targets polyphonic music, its findings on continuous F0 monitoring and low-latency processing are highly relevant. The Automated Music Teacher project cleverly leverages these real-time monitoring concepts but intentionally simplifies the neural network architecture to only process monophonic vocal inputs, thereby securely ensuring the strict project constraint of processing audio in under 50ms is consistently met for instant student feedback.

### 11. Phrase based Raga Recognition - Pitch Distribution Methods (2015)
This 2015 study, published in the International Journal of Computer Applications, fundamentally explores raga recognition through statistical pitch distribution and meticulous phrase analysis. Rather than relying entirely on deep learning black boxes, the method strategically identifies ragas by rigorously analyzing the mathematical probability distributions of different pitches and precisely how frequently certain characteristic phrases occur in an audio sample. By tracking the distribution of notes within the Arohana and Avarohana, the system creates a strong probabilistic map of the performed raga. This classical statistical approach to analyzing pitch sequences is an early precursor to the more advanced neural network grammar validation systems used today. The Automated Music Teacher directly builds upon this highly foundational concept of Arohana-Avarohana rule checking in its Phase 3 grammar validation engine, expanding it heavily from passive recognition to active, real-time detection of forbidden notes.

### 12. Raga Space Visualization using Dimensionality Reduction Techniques (2022)
Presented at the National Conference on Communications, this paper introduces a highly visual and mathematical approach to understanding complex raga relationships through advanced dimensionality reduction. The researchers cleanly map multi-raga datasets into a lower-dimensional visual "raga space" using techniques like t-SNE clustering. By doing so, they definitively demonstrate that highly specific microtonal shruti patterns inherently distinguish different ragas. The study successfully establishes a clear mathematical method for mapping continuous vocal pitch contours directly to discrete, culturally relevant shruti intervals. This visualization and statistical clustering technique is an absolute cornerstone for the microtonal processing requirements of your project. The Automated Music Teacher directly adopts this exact concept to relentlessly compare sung pitches against expected mathematical shruti clusters, forming the absolute core logic of its phase 2 apashruthi diagnostic engine.

### 13. Self-Extracting Feature Maps for Music Audio Analysis (2021)
Published in IEEE Signal Processing Letters, this 2021 study meticulously investigates highly advanced methods for dynamic, automated feature extraction in complex music audio analysis. The authors expertly propose a framework where optimal feature maps are dynamically generated automatically by the network itself, allowing the intelligent system to focus entirely on the most acoustically relevant parts of the signal without relying entirely on traditional, rigid spectral transforms. These powerful self-extracting maps vastly improve the model's overall adaptability to unpredictable variations in timbre and varied recording quality. In the specific context of Carnatic music, where the tonal quality of student voices can vary drastically, adaptable feature extraction is vital. The Automated Music Teacher project incorporates the core philosophy of highly robust, self-learning feature pipelines during its critical Stage 1 extraction process, ensuring that the 1D-DCNN classifier receives properly normalized acoustic data regardless of the student's unique recording environment.

### 14. Singing Voice Separation and Pitch Extraction via DNN (2019)
This 2019 ICASSP paper fundamentally details a highly efficient system for separating vocals from polyphonic tracks and subsequently extracting their exact pitch. The research powerfully utilizes Deep Neural Networks (DNN) alongside adaptive pitch tracking algorithms to securely isolate the singing voice with remarkable speed. The absolute key contribution to the field is the achievement of fast vocal extraction with an incredibly tight latency window of just 30-40ms, resulting in a perfectly clean, isolated acoustic signal. This extremely low-latency processing benchmark is absolutely critical. The Automated Music Teacher project heavily cites this exact capability as definitive proof that its strict sub-50ms real-time latency requirement is technically achievable, utilizing very similar adaptive pitch tracking techniques to securely ensure the student receives instant, visually imperceptible feedback during live vocal practice.

### 15. Vector Space Modelling for Microtonal Music Analysis (2021)
Published in Pattern Recognition Letters, this vital paper strictly presents a rigid mathematical framework specifically designed for analyzing microtonal Indian classical music. The study effectively introduces a robust vector space model purely based on 22-shruti quantization, cleanly mapping highly continuous pitch frequencies directly into discrete, culturally accurate microtonal intervals. Extensive testing on Indian classical datasets demonstrated an impressive accuracy improvement from 88% up to 94% strictly when utilizing this advanced microtonal awareness. This is undeniably one of the absolute most critical foundational papers for the Automated Music Teacher project. The application directly and heavily adopts 22-shruti quantization to reliably achieve ultra-precise apashruthi detection (targeting less than 10 cents of precision). While the original paper heavily focused on passive analytical study, the project innovatively transforms this vector space model directly into an active, real-time pedagogical diagnostic engine that rapidly identifies and visually flags microtonal deviations to the student.
